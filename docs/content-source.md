## 2025 at a Glance

### Who we are

More than half of young adults aged 18 to 34 live with at least one chronic or rare condition, yet this group remains largely overlooked in healthcare, research, and policy. Young adult patients fall between pediatric and adult systems, forced into one or the other instead of receiving care built for this critical stage of life.

Generation Patient was built to close that gap by the people living inside it. Our founder started this organization as a young adult with a chronic illness, and young adult patients still lead it at every level, from our peer facilitators to our policy scholars to our board. We know these systems firsthand, and that is exactly why we can change them.

### How we create change

We work through two levers:

#### Direct support

- Community-led peer support groups
- Fellowship and leadership programming

#### Systems change

- Four Health Policy Priorities:
  - Pharmaceutical Patent Reform
  - Young Adult Clinical Trial Representation
  - Preventing Deceptive Social Media Drug Ads
  - Oversight of AI Chatbots
- Multi-stakeholder roundtables
- Peer-reviewed publications to generate evidence on the gaps in care for young adults with chronic and rare conditions

Our direct support work informs our systems change work, so the lived experience of young adult patients drives lasting reform.

1. **Direct support.** Our peer support groups are a unique model in healthcare. Every group is led by facilitators who are themselves young adults living with a chronic or rare condition, most groups are open to young adults with any diagnosis, and all of them are free. Alongside our peer support work, our fellowship and leadership programming builds the next generation of patient advocates. Our direct support work directly informs the long-term policy change we continue to move forward.
2. **Systems change.** Through our four health policy priorities, our evidence-generation, and our multi-stakeholder roundtables resulting in peer-reviewed publications authored by young adult patients, we put the lived experience of young adult patients at the center of health policy, oversight, and research, from drug pricing and deceptive advertising to clinical trials and AI chatbot and companion oversight.

## Our Work in Depth

### Peer Support

Young adults with chronic and/or rare conditions experience disproportionately high rates of isolation, anxiety, depression, and disruption to education, employment, and enjoyment of everyday life. Despite being at a pivotal stage of life and development, the mental well-being of young adults with chronic medical conditions is frequently overshadowed by our physical health needs. We believe that peer support is one crucial and often overlooked resource that can help reduce isolation and increase feelings of validation, acceptance, and connection in our community.

Peer support is one of the most powerful tools in healthcare, and one that has not yet been taken as seriously as it deserves. We are working to change that on both fronts, by running community-led peer support at scale and by building the research base that demonstrates its value. Since March 2020, we have facilitated more than 650 virtual peer support meetings, all led by facilitators who are themselves young adults living with chronic conditions. Most groups are cross-disease, reflecting the shared experiences of school, career, and relationships that cut across diagnoses.

In 2025, our 77 groups included:

- 23 general peer support groups, open to young adults with any chronic or rare condition
- 18 higher education groups for college and graduate students, including those on medical leave
- 20 groups for young adults with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), our only disease-specific offering
- 15 international groups co-hosted with IBD Patient Support Foundation India and Africa Sickle Cell Organization

Of the 115+ unique patients we served, roughly 85 were new participants and more than 80 returned for multiple groups. In our community's words:

> “There have been weeks when my only laughter came from the peer support meetings.”

> “I show up with my camera off on high-symptom days and still feel completely included. That flexibility keeps me coming back.”

> “I didn't realize how much I was carrying until I heard other people describe exactly what I've been feeling.”

We also advanced the evidence base for this work. With researchers at Boston Children's Hospital, we published “Peer-support needs and experiences of young adults with chronic conditions: A mixed methods study,” with young adult patients serving as co-authors and first author, a standing Generation Patient policy. And we completed a multi-year roundtable funded by a Patient-Centered Outcomes Research Institute (PCORI) Eugene Washington Engagement Award on engaging young adults in comparative effectiveness research for peer support, producing a guidance document, a companion checklist for researchers, a peer support toolkit, proceedings, and a strategic roadmap for patient-led research.

### Health Policy Lab

Our Health Policy Lab ensures the lived experience of young adult patients drives policy reform. The work is led by our Health Policy Scholars, young adults with chronic and rare conditions, and engages Congress and federal agencies including the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA), Federal Trade Commission (FTC), Department of Justice (DOJ), U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO), and Department of Health and Human Services (HHS). In 2025, we focused on four priorities.

#### 1. Patent reform

We helped support the reintroduction of the bipartisan Eliminating Thickets to Increase Competition (ETHIC) Act, led by Senators Welch, Hawley, and Klobuchar, which would limit pharmaceutical companies to asserting a single patent from a thicket of overlapping patents to ensure more timely access to generics and biosimilars. We also advocated against the Patent Eligibility Restoration Act (PERA) and the Promoting and Respecting Economically Vital American Innovation Leadership Act (PREVAIL) Act, which would both create barriers to affordable medicines and prioritize profits for the pharmaceutical industry, and met with dozens of congressional offices. We were also invited to speak at the DOJ and FTC Listening Session on anticompetitive conduct in the pharmaceutical industry, convened under a presidential executive order on lowering drug prices, where our testimony informed the agencies' joint report on pharmaceutical antitrust submitted to the White House.

> “Young adult patients are living with the consequences of patent abuse every day. These policies decide whether people can afford the medications they need to stay alive.”
>
> — Alexander Naum, JD, Policy Manager

#### 2. Deceptive drug advertising

Nearly 90% of young people use social media to find health information, where they face misleading pharmaceutical marketing designed to exploit their vulnerabilities. We have been a leading voice behind the bipartisan Protecting Patients from Deceptive Drug Ads Act, and in 2025 our advocacy contributed to the FDA issuing 60 warning letters and 40 untitled letters to companies engaged in deceptive marketing, including undisclosed influencer promotions.

#### 3. Clinical trials

Trials often lump everyone aged 18 to 64 into one category, obscuring the distinct needs of young adults. We pushed for age-disaggregated trial data, better representation of young adults in study design, improved adverse-event reporting, and rigorous approval standards, meeting routinely with FDA leadership and engaging in the Prescription Drug User Fee Act (PDUFA), Medical Device User Fee Amendments (MDUFA), and Generic Drug User Fee Amendments (GDUFA) processes. These user fee agreements determine how industry fees fund the FDA's review work and are renegotiated with stakeholder input, making them a critical lever for patient-centered reform.

#### 4. AI companion tools

AI tools marketed as emotional or mental health support increasingly target young people, including those with chronic illness. We delivered formal remarks to the FDA's Digital Health Advisory Committee urging that these tools be regulated as medical devices when they claim to address mental health and submitted comments outlining the current regulatory gaps. We are working to require robust clinical evidence before marketing, clear regulatory pathways, and proactive post-market surveillance. This is our newest policy priority and one we will expand significantly in 2026.

### Community Spotlight: Peyton Miles

In March 2022, during her senior year of high school, a viral illness left Peyton Miles with a severe, unremitting headache and, soon, a life lived from bed while her friends attended prom and graduated. “The biggest word I can think of is isolation,” she recalls.

Peyton found Generation Patient on Instagram and joined our peer support groups, where “everyone inherently understands the challenges of living with chronic illness.” She went on to become a Health Policy Scholar and, in 2025, Health Policy Lab Co-Chair, leading our patent reform work alongside co-chair Grace Shults, running community calls and educational sessions on how patent thickets drive up drug prices. Living with chronic migraine and energy-limiting illness, she adapted the work to her body, drafting legislative comments by voice memo to limit screen time.

> “Generation Patient didn't just give me an idea of how I could use my experience to create change; it gave me a purpose and a community, which was so much more than I could've asked for.”

### Convening Experts and Decision-Makers Through Roundtables

In 2025, with support from the Lucile Packard Foundation for Children's Health, we launched our Roundtable on Young Adults with Chronic Medical Conditions, a national learning community bringing young adult patients together with leading clinicians, researchers, and policymakers from top children's hospitals, universities, and research institutions in the U.S. and abroad. Seven sessions tackled the transition from pediatric to adult care, medical trauma and mental health, ableism and ageism in healthcare, peer support, forced poverty and income volatility, clinical trial representation, and AI in health.

Each session will result in a peer-reviewed publication first-authored by a young adult patient. The first three papers were published in 2025, beginning with “Reframing healthcare transition: Systems, relationships, and independence in adolescent-to-adult care,” which identified barriers at the clinic and system levels and called for supports such as transition navigators and tailored care for patients with complex needs.

### Building Global Consensus

Global health institutions lack a shared definition of “young adult,” producing fragmented policies and inconsistent care. In 2025, we launched a first-of-its-kind global initiative to establish the first globally recognized framework for young adults with chronic conditions and non-communicable diseases (NCDs). As a first step, we partnered with the Taiwan Young Patient Association, Africa Sickle Cell Organization, and IBD Patient Support Foundation India to co-host the first international convening dedicated specifically to young adults with chronic conditions, held alongside the UN High-Level Meeting on NCDs and Chronic Conditions in New York City. The convening drew the support of Senator Peter Welch and the Global Mental Health Lead at UNICEF, and the Brazil Ministry of Health participated as well. The published proceedings will lead to the first shared definition of young adults with chronic conditions and NCDs, disseminated across the global healthcare ecosystem in 2026.

### CCYAN: A Global Model

The Crohn's & Colitis Young Adults Network (CCYAN) is Generation Patient's only disease-specific program and a model for what community-led advocacy can achieve. 2025 was its seventh year, and the fellowship has now welcomed fellows from the U.S., India, Ethiopia, the U.K., Bangladesh, Dubai, Greece, Malaysia, and Canada, with 50 alumni to date. In 2025, 8 fellows produced more than 80 articles, videos, and advocacy projects; the CCYAN website drew 17,000+ visits; and every fellow rated the fellowship 5/5. Fellows have gone on to speak at international conferences, publish research, educate clinicians, and build patient networks in their home countries.

> “This fellowship gave me oxygen. It helped me breathe again.”
